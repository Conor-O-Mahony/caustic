Black metal is a style of extreme Metal typified by tremolo-picked riffs, distorted, trebly electric guitars, shrieked vocals, and double-kick drumming and blast beats that also frequently features raw, underproduced sound. Conventional song structures are generally replaced with extensive passages. Black metal's origins trace back to 1980s Speed Metal's blistering sound pioneered by Venom, who adopted unpolished production, anti-Christian and Satanic lyricism, and provocative imagery and stage names. Their sophomore record, Black Metal, is considered to have coined the genre's name.

First wave black metal emerged from a wide range of early extreme metal sounds and didn't always resemble the sound typically associated with modern black metal. The most significant influences came from Thrash Metal scenes, mainly Germany's Teutonic thrash scene, pioneered by Sodom and Kreator, and Belo Horizonte's scene, spearheaded by Sarc√≥fago and Sepultura. Hellhammer doubled down on rawness and created primitive songs with a low-budget lo-fi recording, while Bathory popularized shrieked vocals. Tom G. Warrior and Quorthon, leaders of the aforementioned groups, also cited Discharge's D-Beat as an influence. First wave's popularity faded by the end of the 1980s due to thrash metal's primetime and Death Metal's popularity.

With the 1990s came second wave black metal, spearheaded by the Norwegian Scene. Bands like Darkthrone, Immortal, Burzum, and, at the frontmost, Mayhem created a more repetitive, tremolo riff-focused style with blast beats and double-kick drumming becoming a common practice. These artists also experimented with song structures, rejecting standard verse-chorus pattern, in favor of extended sections. Meanwhile, many scenes and bands started sprouting in different countries around the world, inspired by the successes of Norwegian bands.

By the late 1990s, some projects strayed away from the unpolished, under-produced, repetitious sound. Norwegian bands like Emperor and Enslaved and San Francisco-based group Weakling implemented technically demanding structures and modernized production. In the 2000s, Deathspell Omega built upon this development by intensifying the technicality and adding dense and dissonant sound masses inspired by the avant-garde Classical Music.

Throughout the 2000s and 2010s, black metal featured numerous bands and prominent scenes, notably in the United States, France, Finland, Poland, and Ukraine. Many artists experimented with different genres, such as Traditional Folk Music / Neofolk (Moonsorrow and Agalloch), Psychedelia (Oranssi Pazuzu), Dream Pop / Shoegaze (Alcest and Deafheaven), and Industrial Music (Blut aus Nord and Altar of Plagues). Themes tackled by songwriters also changed over time to include nature, folklore, mythologies, paganism, depression, anxiety, and philosophy. Some genres, even those outside of metal, took in black metal influence to create "blackened" styles, such as Blackened Crust and Black Ambient.

Blaze in the Northern Sky is the second studio album by Norwegian black metal band Darkthrone.

Read More

 It was recorded at Creative Studios in August 1991 and released on 26 February 1992 by Peaceville Records. Apart from earlier rehearsal recordings between March 1991 and the Norwegian summer holidays (later released as a demo compilation in 2016) the record contained the band's first official black metal recordings and is considered a classic within the genre. It was the first album of what fans dub the "Unholy Trinity", the other albums being Under a Funeral Moon and Transilvanian Hunger. It was the last album to feature bassist Dag Nilsen. In 2020, the album was put on the National Library of Norway's permanent exhibition due to its significance to Norwegian culture and as a starting point for Norwegian black metal. Source: Wikipedia
